# Tenet Audit: Music and Friendship (John & Reza)

**Tenet:** Music is important for friendships — especially John and Reza. (Structural context: the acts carry Beatles references, John becomes The Walrus, and the regime's control system is SONIC — so music-as-connection stands opposed to sound-as-control.)

**Verdict up front:** Music is *everywhere* as texture and joke, and John/Reza is the most music-saturated relationship in the book — but almost none of it is load-bearing. Music scores their scenes; it never *carries* one. Their four biggest emotional beats (bunk-bed confession 528, violinist reveal 530, hand-holding 614, farewell/pub chase 622–623) are all music-silent. Meanwhile the counterpoint machinery (Harmony/Oohashi vs. bangers-on-tape) is fully built and wired — it just never gets switched on in a single audible moment. The good news: the fixes are one-line insertions, because the setups already exist.

## 1. MUSIC ON THE PAGE

**The air-guitar frame (the book's musical spine)**
- **19** — John's introduction is a full air-guitar fantasy: "The crowd roars. The music picks up, the guitar a wailing discordance… John Blard reads the crowd and pulls a filthy lick on his air guitar" — then he catches himself in the mirror and takes his headphones out. Music = John's private, embarrassing inner life.
- **617–618** — The ending repeats this passage *verbatim* as John, in the Walrus mask, addresses the nation. The fantasy becomes real. The book's strongest existing music structure — the imagined gig is the one frequency the state never touched.

**Illegal music / the post-2001 ban**
- **31** — John's spare room hides "his illegal post 2001 mix tape" (Jones calls him "the pretentious wanker"). Restated at **212**.
- **105** — Three Beers Blard mimes into furniture polish to "technically illegal but even better for it music."
- **74–75** — Realist ideology is anti-new-music nostalgia: "no more scary teenagers with their new music"; AI slop killed songwriting ("The question stopped being who wrote a song but what wrote a song").
- **548** — "By the Way is an illegal banger" — John frets about the ban *while storming a government agency*.

**John & Jones's drunken night (the Jones-side precedent)**
- **119–124** — The one scene where music actively bonds two friends: The Cure, Brimful of Asha, Moloko, "screaming the lyrics of Drinking in LA into a spatula."
- **128** — "Radio Off. Oasis stubbornly refused to cease. 'Won't work for you.'" (Even friendly music arrives through the *state's* embedded radio.)

**Jones and music:** 78 (letterbox singing threat), 255 (funeral song in her life-flash), 257 (the state weaponises footage of Jones *singing* the Welsh anthem), 418 (post-harmonisation she "mouthed the words to herself" — her voice stolen and reprogrammed).

**State muzak & nostalgia-pop as crowd control:** 138 (Lighthouse Family over the Pulpifier platform), 164 (Two Become One "filtered through a can of soup"), 374–384 (the Denunciation scored like a festival: Angels, Livin' La Vida Loca, Higher Love), 419 (the Goober Cleber Collective), 449 (Vetko's coup broadcast opens with a skipping record player playing the national anthem).

**Misc:** 353 (AI917's Attenborough remix), 478 (Parkinson-theme door knock), 489 ("sassy saxophone"), 523 (NIMBY victory party: boom boxes, Oops Upside Your Head, Reza teaching Ethel dance moves), 578/615 ("Big DJ Dans Decks Mobile"), 603 ("All it would take was some loud music and a hammer").

**Assessment.** Music is Reza's *costume* — tapes, bangers, car speakers — and it soundtracks every logistical scene they share. Two genuinely structural touches exist: (a) **music-choice-as-consent** — John joins the plot by asking to pick the music (245) and Reza re-recruits him with exactly that offer (293); (b) **the rescue announced by music** cutting through a Drone's alarm (496). But the *emotional* freight is carried entirely by banter and shared danger. No single song recurs between them. And the **violinist reveal (530) is connected to no actual music whatsoever** — the book's most musical character turns out to be a concert violinist's son and it explains only his accent; Reza never touches an instrument; the reveal is never referenced again.

## 3. THE COUNTERPOINT: music vs. Harmony

**The control system is sonic, thoroughly:** Harmony masts / Oohashi nodes "programming people to be peaceful" (14–15, 44); the node's "thum thum thum thum" inside Jones's head the night she's taken (186, 197); Baker's office is a *recording studio of control* — acoustic foam, mixers, six hanging speakers, Baker humming "bits and pieces of songs sewn together that should never have been" (267–269); "Ah what beautiful music we'll make" before harmonising Jones (203); harmonised Jones is "a hypersonic puppet… She must play her part in the melody. Her note must play" (219, 233–234); Roger is controlled by a **whistle** (555–556, 606, 611) and hears the node as "singing, it was beautiful" (544); embedded radios deliver unskippable updates and won't switch off for the wrong voice (70, 128). The state even runs pop music as an arm of control — the Denunciation is DJ'd (374–384, 419) — and its founding ideology is a ban on new music (74–75).

**Where friendly music already stands against control-sound (implicit, uncashed):**
1. **496** — the most direct image in the book: John pinned by a Drone, "yet faintly, so faintly there was music, blending in with the alarm." Friendship is *heard as music inside the state's siren* before it's seen.
2. **532** — Maurice states the thesis as a joke: "get young Bogglehoffer to Harmony, **turn that shite off, play that funky music** and bobs your uncle."
3. **548** — contraband music as the rebellion's soundtrack en route to the Ministry.
4. **439** — the Denunciation crowd's awakening framed sonically: anger "fighting against the ever present hum of the Oohashi node… **Discordance**."
5. **595** — John terminates Harmony: "The omni present hum stopped." The state's frequency dies into silence — and nothing fills it. Maurice's "play that funky music" is left unpaid.
6. **19/617–618** — the air-guitar bookend: the broadcast that stops the riots is a man privately hearing music the state never programmed.

The opposition is *implicit five separate times* — the book has never once made it **audible in the same room**.

## 4. HARDENING SUGGESTIONS

1. **Give them one recurring song, seeded at the meeting (224–225), paid at the rescue (496–497).** The Passenger is already playing when Reza introduces himself — thematically perfect (John spends the whole book being driven by other people). At the rescue, swap A Flock of Seagulls for The Passenger: John recognises the song *inside the Drone's alarm* before he recognises the car — he knows it's Reza by the music. One recurring song converts fifteen scattered needle-drops into a friendship theme.

2. **Put music inside the bunk-bed scene and cash the violinist reveal in the same breath (526–530).** After "Night Reza. / Night John," in the dark, Reza hums something to fill the silence and it isn't a banger — it's something bowed, classical, from before; John clocks it; Reza deflects ("my dad, innit"). Optionally one more line making the tape obsession *about the dad*: everything Reza has of him is what got taped before the Tumult. Suddenly the cassette at 291/305/547 is grief-inventory, not set-dressing — and the fake-roadman disguise (498, 529) has a heart under it.

3. **A callback at the hand-holding (613–614).** As they wait to die, give them four bars: one starts humming their song (badly), the other joins (worse). This happens ~5 snippets after the hum of Harmony stops at 595: the first music in a newly silent Britain is two frightened friends out of tune, holding hands. It also sets up 622, where Reza already whistles — make *that* whistle the same tune, so the pub chase at 623 lands on a trained ear.

4. **Pay off "Can I pick the music?" at the pub chase (245 → 293 → 623).** The motif of music-choice-as-consent is already built. When John hurls himself across the Mustang's bonnet at 623 — "Do you fancy going to the pub? …**You can pick the music.**" Six words; the last line of the friendship rhymes with its first.

5. **Let Reza's violin exist once, physically, before the reveal.** Doris's junk-bazaar Shop (327, 473–474) is the natural place for a dusty violin among the taxidermy. Reza plucking one string dead in tune and walking away without comment (473) is a two-line plant that makes 530 a payoff instead of a punchline — and makes teaching-Ethel-dance-moves (523) and the Walkman-lending (452) read as a man who processes everything through music because he was *raised in it*.

**The single best place to make music-vs-Harmony audible once, without preaching: snippet 595.** "John pressed enter. The omni present hum stopped." The book spends 600 snippets establishing the hum and one sentence killing it — into dead silence. Maurice already promised the punchline at 532, and Reza — the man with a tape in every pocket — is standing in that Control Room (569, 572, 585). Let the first sound in unprogrammed Britain be his tinny, illegal, post-2001 banger clicking on in the sudden quiet. No speech, no thesis — one sentence of contraband music where the state's frequency used to be. The Walrus broadcast at 617–618 then inherits it: the air guitar was never a fantasy, it was the resistance's actual weapon.
