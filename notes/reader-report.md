# Reader Panel Report — *Parliament of the Snake*

Three reader personas read the full manuscript (638 snippets) in four quarters; an agency gatekeeper read the opening quarter only. Snippet numbers below match your editing tool.

---

## 1. The verdicts

### The comedy lifer
Started sold on the voice — the goat-sacrifice cold open (1-3), Wozzy the drone, and the Elleray Palm farce engine read as "the real deal, a genuinely good Pratchett/Adams ear" — but flagged uneven joke density (the joke-free lore dump at 72-77, repeated checkpoint scenes) and a narrator who can't resist "one more quip a beat too soon" at emotional turns. Q2 was called "the strongest stretch so far" structurally: Jones's death (252-256) is "a proper gut-punch/chapter-break combo" where whimsy and horror finally fuse, though the drugged abduction gets blunted by comic asides. By Q3 the runners (Reza's slang, the toilet gag, 90s pop-culture drops) were "running on repetition rather than escalation," but the Drone tape (411-418) and Walrus broadcast (436-439) proved comedy could soften the reader up for the gut-punch instead of fighting it. Q4 verdict: the book is strongest "either in full farce or full bleakness" — the NIMBY Town Hall (508-516) and Delta/Cesira's deaths (607-610) are the peaks — while the long ThinkTank assault (548-585) trades comic timing for generic action, and ending on raw author's notes (625-638) is "a rough way to close a reading session."

### The dystopia reader
Q1: right ingredients for the genre — Wozzy, the Julie-purged-like-spam-in-the-chat-log satire (97-98, 106, 124), Roger's banal complicity — but the plot engine was "almost entirely opaque at the 160-snippet mark" and Elleray Palm's Australian shtick wore thin fast. Q2 delivered "the book's best dystopian material so far": the Harmony conceit shows its teeth in the hill abduction (186-198), Julie's possession, and the Ministry of Peace tour, while the Reza heist-comedy stretch "reads tonally closer to a heist comedy" and dilutes the dread. Q3 was where "the dread mostly wins where it matters" — the Jones torture tape (413-418) and Baker's frequency-44 monologue (461) finally make the mind-control premise cohere as world logic — though the connective tissue (tunnel crawl, double psychic-shop, Sue) is bloated. Q4 "cashes the checks the first three-quarters wrote" (the prison block at 581, Vetko harmonising Baker at 555-557), but the constant joke rate through the siege, an unexplained Lovecraft swerve (603-605), and the dissolve into author's notes softened stakes "right when the genre needs them hardest."

### The literary reader
Consistently framed the book as "a writer with real ability boxed into a sitcom chassis that won't let scenes sit still." Q1's proof points: Bollo's grave (88), Julie's fear (14-18, 65), Roger's midnight texts (131). Q2 split "cleanly into two registers, and only one of them is working" — Erica's death (179-183) is "the one place the novel stops performing and just sits with a person," while Jones's death-flashback punchline ("I thought there would be more") read as "the book flinching" — notably the exact line the comedy lifer loved. Q3: the drone footage and Roger's harmonisation are "the strongest, darkest writing" in the manuscript, but "it costs a lot of patience to get there" through padding. Q4: the battle was "mostly a slog" for this reader — four major deaths get "maybe a paragraph of real weight each before the prose cuts to a quip" — redeemed by the John/Reza bunk-bed scene (525-528, "the best-written material in the whole batch"), the Town Hall satire, and the quiet coda (619-623). Would put the manuscript down at the unmarked switch to scratch notes at 626.

### The gatekeeper (opening quarter only)
> "Yes, request the full manuscript, but flag to the author that Part One needs trimming. The voice is genuinely distinctive and commercial — this reads like it knows exactly what shelf it wants to sit on (Pratchett/Fforde/Black Mirror-with-jokes) and mostly earns the comparison. The two protagonists are vivid and the satire has teeth; by the end of this excerpt there's a real engine (Julie vanished, men in her house, Elleray Palm's sinister recruitment) that justifies reading on to see if the threads actually converge."

Their headline concerns: ~80 of 160 snippets pass before the inciting mystery moves; the regime infodump (72-77) "stops the story cold right after a strong cliffhanger"; two near-identical checkpoint sequences (53-61, 134-142); a large ensemble that risks diffusing whose novel this is; and the need for reassurance that the plot payoff "is plotted, not just more absurdist scaffolding."

## 2. Where all readers agree

### Consensus highlights — protect these
- **Wozzy the drone (45-48, 102-104, 159-160, 596).** All three personas plus the gatekeeper singled him out: comedy that does surveillance-state character work at once. His fine-recital while stabbing Baker (596) is a climax highlight for the comedy lifer.
- **Erica's "At first / In the end" death (179-183).** Called the best sustained prose of Q2 by all three readers, in near-identical terms: played straight, no gag, earns its grief.
- **The Drone tape of Jones's abduction and programming (411-418).** Unanimous strongest writing of Q3 — the flat institutional drone voice over torture, comedy switched off and the shift earned.
- **Roger's arc as banal complicity.** His private apology DMs to Julie (130-131) landed for everyone in Q1; his forced harmonisation (456-463) landed for everyone in Q3 as an earned, bleak reversal.
- **Jones's death scene structurally (245-256).** All readers agree the farce-into-bomb collision and the "PART TWO" hard cut work (though they split on the final line — see section 3).
- **Vetko's "You were never that important" / Cesira off the roof (609-610).** Unanimous gut-punch: undramatic, subverts the final-showdown cliché, makes Vetko genuinely dangerous.
- **The NIMBY Town Hall / John's speech (508-521).** Comedy lifer's "comic high point of the quarter"; dystopia reader's sharp satire of British complacency; literary reader's "sharpest satire in the section... it earns its comic excess because there's an actual argument under it."
- **Bollo's grave (88) and Julie's fear (14-18, 40, 65).** Early proof, cited by all, that the book can do quiet, ungarnished feeling.

### Consensus problems — the highest-signal fixes
- **The reflex quip that lands on top of an emotional or tension peak.** This is the single most repeated criticism across all twelve reads. Named instances: the "Frognage" quip after the smashed frog (156-157), the Deflated Jones pun after Erica's death (183), the Knight Rider aside mid-abduction (189), the stationery-cupboard firework gag after John's rage monologue (422), the "mandem" bit seconds before the broadcast (432, 436), Elleray Palm's "land down under" pun-death (598 — misfired for dystopia and literary readers), the fake "THE END" gag straight after two major deaths (618).
- **The regime backstory infodump (72-77).** Joke-free, proper-noun-saturated, kills momentum after a cliffhanger — flagged by all three personas and the gatekeeper.
- **Snippet 272 (and 271).** The ALL-CAPS think-tank passage reads to two readers as unedited author notes accidentally left in the manuscript. Needs cutting or clear reworking before anyone else sees it.
- **Snippets 625-638.** All three readers hit the same wall: the narrative dissolves into raw brainstorming, outline bullets, alternate dialogue, and a cipher, with no marker separating prose from scratch. Every reader said this is where they'd put the book down.
- **Repeated structures without escalation.** The two near-identical checkpoint sequences (53-61 vs 134-142 — three readers + gatekeeper); the Rule 17a Watch-meeting bickering running four beats too long (5-18); the second Rosemary Doris shop visit repeating the first almost verbatim (326-337 vs 471-476); the "Walrus, what now?" beat run four times near-verbatim (561, 569, 578, 583).
- **The long ThinkTank assault (548-585).** Comedy lifer and literary reader both sagged: wave-after-wave action that trades the book's voice for choreography; dystopia reader found the constant joke rate undercutting momentum through the same stretch.
- **Elleray Palm's one-joke Australian shtick in Q1 (89-97).** Dystopia and literary readers both wore out by his second scene (the comedy lifer liked the farce structure but flagged the "bastardasaurus" riff at 95 as tryhard). Note: all readers agree the character *pays off* later — the window-smash reveal (282-288) and the death scene's affection are earned — the problem is Q1 dosage.
- **Proper-noun overload.** The faction dump (72-77, 106), the warehouse influx of new names (293-299), and the acronym burst (292-296) lost every reader who commented on them.
- **Broadcast mechanics (426-430, 448-449).** A mile of range vs. a live festival crowd vs. "every household in the country" — the comedy lifer couldn't square it and the plot leans on it.

## 3. Where they split

These are trade-offs, not defects — different target readers pull in different directions.

- **"I thought there would be more" (255-256).** The comedy lifer: "a proper gut-punch... the rare moment whimsy and horror actually fuse." The literary reader: "the book flinching... the death that most needed to be given room and wasn't." If your primary shelf is Pratchett/Adams, keep it; if you want review-page respectability, this is the line the literary crowd will quote against you.
- **MmmMartin (79-81, 91, 148, 321-329).** Comedy lifer: "nasty, well-aimed workplace satire... uncomfortable in the right way." Dystopia and literary readers: over-explained, "dated laddish banter" that doesn't land as commentary. Decide whether the bit is aimed satire or texture — as written it reads differently to different rooms.
- **The bull-cum gag (26-27).** Comedy lifer: filthy but character-revealing, real comic timing. Literary reader: "broad, juvenile, over-explained... stops a scene rather than propelling it."
- **The Ministry of Peace department tour (273-278).** Dystopia reader: "the sharpest satire in the quarter — bureaucratic euphemism doing real Orwellian work." Comedy lifer and literary reader: a naming gag that needs three names and gets six-plus, with diminishing returns. Possible resolution both camps could accept: keep the device, cut it to the best three doors.
- **Elleray Palm's death (593-598).** Comedy lifer: the quarter's proof that comedy-into-pathos works — "the comedy earned the tears." Dystopia reader: the pun exit line "turns grief into a Monty Python outro." Literary reader: "the joke and the grief... largely cancel both." Two of three readers want the pun cut and the feeling kept; one thinks the pun *is* the feeling.
- **Pete the abandoned monitor (404-410).** Comedy lifer: comedy "curdles cleverly into something closer to horror" — a highlight. Dystopia and literary readers: the joke density crowds out both the pathos and the creepiness. Same scene, opposite verdicts on whether the gags are load-bearing.
- **The MoM's incompetent security (388-393).** Dystopia reader: a plausible Brazil-style rot joke, but played so broadly it briefly undercuts the omnipresent-surveillance stakes. Comedy lifer: Sue is a clean Discworld-style one-scene grotesque. Literary reader: exhausting by the fourth endearment.
- **The fake "THE END" / "THE ACTUAL END" (617-623).** Comedy lifer: "a good authorial wink that's also true to the character." Dystopia reader: mistimed whiplash seconds after major deaths. Literary reader: momentarily confusing but loved the coda it frames. The wink can probably stay if it's moved a beat further from the deaths.
- **Supernatural swerves (H'gok, 603-605; Baker on the ceiling, 589-592; Vetko catching the knife, 609).** Comedy lifer: H'gok is "very Adams in its shrug" — a highlight. Dystopia and literary readers: an unmotivated genre break that blurs the book's rules right at the climax. Vetko's implied inhumanity got the opposite split — the readers who disliked H'gok *loved* the knife-catch as a gut-punch but want groundwork for it.

## 5. Top 10 fixes by reader impact

1. **Separate prose from scratch material at 625-638 (and fix 271-272).** All readers hit a hard stop; two flagged 272 as a draft artifact mid-manuscript. Nothing else matters if a reader thinks the book just fell apart.
2. **Institute a "no quip after the peak" pass on the ~8 named sabotage points:** 156-157, 183, 189, 422, 432/436, 563, 577, 598, 618. This is the single most repeated note across all twelve reads.
3. **Break up or dramatise the regime infodump (72-77)** and thin the proper-noun clumps (106, 292-299) — every reader lost the faction map, and it sits on top of a cliffhanger.
4. **Cut the ThinkTank assault (548-585) by a third:** collapse the attack waves, cut the "Walrus, what now?" beat to two uses (561, 569, 578, 583), signpost the cross-cuts to the Central Node (544-557), and cut or pay off the torture-room tour (570-575).
5. **Trim Q1's front half:** the Watch meeting's Rule 17a loop (7-18) by 3-4 beats, and differentiate or merge the two checkpoint sequences (53-61 vs 135-142) — gatekeeper's headline concern.
6. **Ration Elleray Palm's Q1 shtick (89-97) and decide on his death pun (598):** two of three readers want "land down under" cut and the "We're mates aren't we Blard?" feeling kept; the payoff scenes later prove the character earns his place.
7. **Fix the broadcast-range logic (426-430, 448-449)** — a mile vs. the nation is the one plot mechanic no reader could square, and the climax of Act 2 hangs on it.
8. **Decide the rules for the supernatural (589-592, 603-605, 609):** ground Vetko's inhumanity earlier, and either seed the Eldritch department before the climax or cut H'gok — two of three readers felt the book break its own rules at the worst moment.
9. **Cut the doubled Rosemary Doris visit (471-476) or make the repetition mean something** — as written it reads as the book repeating a bit, and the ambiguity (same magical shop? franchise gag?) undercuts the joke it's going for.
10. **Compress the list-shaped gags that outstay their welcome:** MoP department tour to three doors (273-278), Denunciation merch stalls to three items (373-379), digit-string spam to one full rendering (98, 106), Sue's endearments (389-391), and the tunnel crawl's pee-joke padding (352-362).

Honourable mentions from single readers worth a look: flag the Stanley continuity reveal (187 → 273) so it doesn't read as coincidence; clarify Julie's fate after 256 (intentional mystery vs. dropped thread); fix the Delta Ilos/Isol name inconsistency (341, 372, 424, 463); nail down MoP/Madhouse/ThinkTank geography (530-590); and give Reza's posh-kid reveal (529) one seed of foreshadowing so it reads as a reveal rather than a retcon.
